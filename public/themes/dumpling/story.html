Our story

由來From Guangzhou to your block.

師傅The chef.

Chef Wong Kam-Wai started folding har gow in his grandmother's kitchen in Guangzhou at age nine. He spent eighteen years moving through Hong Kong tea houses before bringing the craft to Your Store Name.

Every morning at 5am, he and his team start with cold water and flour. By 11am, the carts are full. By 3pm, they're empty. We don't fold ahead. We don't freeze.

手藝The craft.

A har gow has 12 pleats. A xiao long bao has 18. A siu mai is open at the top, with 32 small folds around the side. These aren't traditions for the sake of tradition — each shape was designed for a specific filling, a specific cook time, a specific bite.

"You can taste the difference between 12 pleats and 9. Trust me."

茶樓The room.

Lacquered red columns. Bamboo screens. Bottomless pots of tea. The dim sum carts roll between 11am and 3pm seven days a week, just like a Hong Kong tea house in the 1970s.

You can come for an hour-long lunch, or stay all afternoon with a pot of pu-erh and a stack of bamboo baskets. Both are welcome. Both are how we like it.
