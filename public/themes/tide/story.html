The story

Six years between the boats and the bar.

It started as a chalkboard.

In 2019, we opened a 14-seat oyster bar in a former laundromat. There was no kitchen, no menu — just a slab of marble, a bag of ice, and a chalkboard listing whatever the morning's boats had brought up.

Six years later, we're a little bigger, but the chalkboard is still the heart of the room. What was alive yesterday is what's on tonight.

Our boats

A short list of fishers we trust.

We work with a handful of small-vessel operators in BC, Washington and southern Alaska. Day-boat sablefish from Sointula. Spot prawns from Pender Harbour. Live Dungeness from Comox.

If we wouldn't buy a fish from someone, we won't put it on the chalkboard.

About the oysters.

We carry between 10 and 16 varieties on any given night, all from the Pacific Northwest. Most come from family-run farms in Baynes Sound, Cortes Island, Read Island and Hood Canal — places we've visited, owners we know.

Ask the shucker. They'll walk you from the lightest, sweetest petite Kusshi all the way down to a deep, briny Sawmill Bay.

The room.

One marble bar. A wood grill in the open kitchen. Forty seats at small tables. Big windows facing the street. We don't take groups over six because the room can't hold the noise — and we'd rather you hear yourself think.
